## Abstract

This document presents the current proof-oriented framework of the Lange Number Line project for the Collatz conjecture.

The central strategy is not to infer universal convergence from high numerical verification, record trajectories or average statistical drift. Instead, the problem is reduced to a precise sufficient statement:

> For every integer \(n>1\), some Collatz iterate is strictly smaller than \(n\).

Once this first-descent statement is established for every positive integer, strong induction implies that every positive integer eventually reaches \(1\).

The framework contains:

1. an exact accelerated odd-number map;
2. a proven immediate-descent lemma for odd numbers congruent to \(1 \pmod 4\);
3. an exact growth-corridor lemma for numbers with trailing binary ones;
4. an exact affine representation of every finite Collatz segment;
5. a symbolic residue-tree strategy;
6. a finite certificate requirement with an independent checker.

At the present stage, the local lemmas and computational verification are established, while the universal symbolic residue-covering certificate remains incomplete. Accordingly, this document describes a proof framework and the remaining proof obligation; it does not yet claim a completed proof of the Collatz conjecture.

# 1. The Collatz Map

For every positive integer \(n\), define the standard Collatz map

\[
C(n)=
\begin{cases}
n/2, & n\equiv 0 \pmod 2,\\[4pt]
3n+1, & n\equiv 1 \pmod 2.
\end{cases}
\]

The Collatz conjecture states:

\[
\forall n\in\mathbb N_{>0}\;\exists k\ge 0:
C^k(n)=1.
\]

Because every even number immediately maps to a smaller integer, the essential problem may be reduced to odd integers.

For an odd integer \(n>1\), define the accelerated odd map

\[
T(n)=\frac{3n+1}{2^{a(n)}},
\]

where

\[
a(n)=v_2(3n+1)
\]

is the greatest exponent such that \(2^{a(n)}\mid 3n+1\).

The map \(T\) sends every odd positive integer to the next odd integer in its ordinary Collatz trajectory.

---

# 2. Sufficient Proof Target

## Theorem 1 — First-Descent Reduction

Assume that

\[
\forall n>1\;\exists k\ge 1:
C^k(n)<n.
\]

Then every positive integer eventually reaches \(1\).

### Proof

Proceed by strong induction on \(n\).

The statement is true for \(n=1\).

Assume that every positive integer \(m<n\) reaches \(1\). By the first-descent assumption, there exists a \(k\ge1\) such that

\[
C^k(n)=m<n.
\]

By the induction hypothesis, \(m\) reaches \(1\). Therefore \(n\) also reaches \(1\).

Hence every positive integer reaches \(1\). \(\square\)

---

## Corollary 1

It is sufficient to prove that every odd integer \(n>1\) has an accelerated iterate below its starting value:

\[
\forall n>1,\ n\text{ odd},\ \exists k\ge1:
T^k(n)<n.
\]

Even integers already descend under division by two.

# 3. Immediate Descent for One Half of All Odd Classes

## Lemma 1 — The \(1 \pmod 4\) Descent Lemma

Let \(n>1\) be odd and satisfy

\[
n\equiv1\pmod4.
\]

Then

\[
T(n)<n.
\]

### Proof

Write

\[
n=4q+1.
\]

Then

\[
3n+1=3(4q+1)+1=12q+4=4(3q+1).
\]

Therefore

\[
v_2(3n+1)\ge2.
\]

It follows that

\[
T(n)
=
\frac{3n+1}{2^{v_2(3n+1)}}
\le
\frac{3n+1}{4}.
\]

For \(n>1\),

\[
\frac{3n+1}{4}<n
\]

because

\[
3n+1<4n
\iff
1<n.
\]

Thus

\[
T(n)<n.
\]

\(\square\)

---

## Consequence

Every odd integer in the residue class

\[
n\equiv1\pmod4
\]

has immediate accelerated descent.

The unresolved initial branch is therefore

\[
n\equiv3\pmod4.
\]

For such numbers,

\[
v_2(3n+1)=1,
\]

and the first accelerated step is

\[
T(n)=\frac{3n+1}{2}>n.
\]

Thus difficult trajectories begin in the \(3\pmod4\) branch.

# 4. Deterministic Growth Corridors

Some odd integers pass through several consecutive accelerated steps with only one division by two. These are not random exceptions; they are generated by an exact binary structure.

## Lemma 2 — Trailing-One Growth-Corridor Lemma

Let

\[
n=2^r m-1,
\]

where \(r\ge1\) and \(m\) is a positive integer.

Then for every integer \(j\) satisfying

\[
0\le j<r,
\]

the accelerated iterates satisfy

\[
T^j(n)=3^j2^{r-j}m-1.
\]

Moreover, during the first \(r-1\) accelerated steps,

\[
v_2\!\left(3T^j(n)+1\right)=1.
\]

### Proof

For \(j=0\),

\[
T^0(n)=n=2^rm-1,
\]

which agrees with the formula.

Assume that for some \(j<r-1\),

\[
T^j(n)=3^j2^{r-j}m-1.
\]

Then

\[
3T^j(n)+1
=
3\left(3^j2^{r-j}m-1\right)+1
\]

and therefore

\[
3T^j(n)+1
=
3^{j+1}2^{r-j}m-2.
\]

Factor out \(2\):

\[
3T^j(n)+1
=
2\left(3^{j+1}2^{r-j-1}m-1\right).
\]

Because \(j<r-1\), the quantity

\[
3^{j+1}2^{r-j-1}m
\]

is even, so the expression inside the parentheses is odd. Hence exactly one factor of \(2\) is removed:

\[
v_2\!\left(3T^j(n)+1\right)=1.
\]

Therefore

\[
T^{j+1}(n)
=
3^{j+1}2^{r-j-1}m-1.
\]

The result follows by induction. \(\square\)

---

## Interpretation

An odd integer with \(r\) trailing one-bits in binary lies in the residue family

\[
n\equiv-1\pmod{2^r}.
\]

Such a number is forced through a deterministic growth corridor before a larger power-of-two division can occur.

This explains why difficult trajectories frequently appear in residue classes such as

\[
n\equiv7\pmod8,
\]

\[
n\equiv15\pmod{16},
\]

\[
n\equiv31\pmod{32},
\]

and, more generally,

\[
n\equiv-1\pmod{2^r}.
\]

The lemma describes the growth mechanism exactly. It does not by itself prove that the later division is always sufficiently strong to create descent.

# 5. Exact Affine Representation of a Collatz Segment

Let

\[
n_0=n
\]

and define

\[
n_i=T(n_{i-1})
=
\frac{3n_{i-1}+1}{2^{a_i}},
\]

where

\[
a_i=v_2(3n_{i-1}+1).
\]

Define the cumulative halving exponent

\[
S_k=\sum_{i=1}^{k}a_i.
\]

## Lemma 3 — Exact Affine Iteration Identity

After \(k\) accelerated odd steps,

\[
T^k(n)=\frac{3^kn+C_k}{2^{S_k}},
\]

where

\[
C_0=0
\]

and

\[
C_{k+1}=3C_k+2^{S_k}.
\]

### Proof

For \(k=0\),

\[
T^0(n)=n=\frac{3^0n+C_0}{2^{S_0}}
\]

with \(C_0=0\) and \(S_0=0\).

Assume

\[
T^k(n)=\frac{3^kn+C_k}{2^{S_k}}.
\]

Then

\[
T^{k+1}(n)
=
\frac{3T^k(n)+1}{2^{a_{k+1}}}.
\]

Substituting the induction hypothesis gives

\[
T^{k+1}(n)
=
\frac{
3(3^kn+C_k)+2^{S_k}
}{
2^{S_k+a_{k+1}}
}.
\]

Thus

\[
T^{k+1}(n)
=
\frac{
3^{k+1}n+3C_k+2^{S_k}
}{
2^{S_{k+1}}
}.
\]

Defining

\[
C_{k+1}=3C_k+2^{S_k}
\]

proves the identity. \(\square\)

---

# 6. Exact Descent Criterion

By the affine identity,

\[
T^k(n)<n
\]

is equivalent to

\[
\frac{3^kn+C_k}{2^{S_k}}<n.
\]

Multiplying by \(2^{S_k}\) gives

\[
3^kn+C_k<2^{S_k}n.
\]

Therefore descent occurs exactly when

\[
\left(2^{S_k}-3^k\right)n>C_k.
\]

This separates the proof obligation into two parts:

1. the multiplicative condition

\[
2^{S_k}>3^k;
\]

2. control of the positive affine correction term \(C_k\).

Define the drift margin

\[
D_k=S_k-k\log_2 3.
\]

When

\[
D_k>0,
\]

the multiplicative factor satisfies

\[
\frac{3^k}{2^{S_k}}<1.
\]

However, positive drift alone is not sufficient, because the affine term \(C_k\) remains positive. A complete proof must establish the full inequality

\[
\left(2^{S_k}-3^k\right)n>C_k
\]

for every integer in every unresolved residue family.

# 7. Residue Families as Exact Symbolic Branches

For a fixed finite valuation word

\[
(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_k),
\]

the integers producing that word form an exact congruence class modulo a power of two.

A symbolic node is represented by:

\[
n\equiv r\pmod{2^b},
\]

together with the exact affine iterate

\[
T^k(n)=\frac{3^kn+C_k}{2^{S_k}}.
\]

Each node is split according to the next value

\[
a_{k+1}=v_2(3T^k(n)+1).
\]

The resulting structure is called the **LNL symbolic residue tree**.

Every node represents infinitely many integers, not a finite sample.

---

# 8. Valid Closure Conditions

A residue-tree leaf may be declared closed only if one of the following conditions is proved.

## Closure A — Uniform Descent

For every integer \(n\) represented by the leaf,

\[
T^k(n)<n.
\]

Using the affine identity, this requires the exact inequality

\[
\left(2^{S_k}-3^k\right)n>C_k
\]

throughout the complete residue family.

---

## Closure B — Reduction to a Smaller Certified Family

The symbolic iterate enters a residue family whose members have already been proved to descend.

This reduction must use exact integer identities, not only equal residue signatures or statistical similarity.

---

## Closure C — Finite Exceptional Set

The symbolic inequality holds above an explicit threshold, leaving only finitely many smaller values.

Each remaining value must then be checked exactly and recorded in the certificate.

---

## Non-Closure

A branch may not be declared solved merely because:

- many tested values descend;
- its average drift is negative;
- no counterexample has been observed;
- it resembles another branch modulo a fixed modulus;
- its trajectories appear to merge computationally;
- its members have been tested to a large finite bound.

Every uncovered residue family remains an open branch.

---

# 9. Finite-Certificate Theorem

## Theorem 2 — Conditional Completion by Residue Covering

Suppose there exists a finite symbolic residue tree satisfying all of the following conditions:

1. its root covers every odd integer greater than \(1\);
2. every branch split is an exact and exhaustive congruence partition;
3. every leaf is closed by uniform descent, reduction to a previously certified smaller family, or a finite explicitly verified exception set;
4. every affine identity and inequality is verified by an independent exact checker.

Then the Collatz conjecture is true.

### Proof

Every odd integer \(n>1\) belongs to exactly one root-to-leaf residue branch.

Because every leaf is validly closed, there exists a finite accelerated iterate satisfying

\[
T^k(n)<n.
\]

Thus every odd positive integer eventually falls below its starting value.

Every even positive integer falls immediately to a smaller integer under division by two.

Therefore every integer \(n>1\) eventually reaches an integer smaller than \(n\).

By Theorem 1 and strong induction, every positive integer reaches \(1\). \(\square\)

# 10. Relation to the Lange Number Line Model

The Lange Number Line model represents a Collatz trajectory as two structural branches around a turning or meeting point.

For a starting number \(n\):

- the left branch records the forward trajectory from \(n\);
- the right branch records the already known suffix toward \(1\);
- their first exact merge identifies a structural closure point;
- the turning point belongs to both branches;
- confirmation may become visible one operation after the causal meeting event.

This geometric interpretation is useful for:

- visualizing trajectory merges;
- identifying repeated suffixes;
- constructing exact trajectory databases;
- separating newly evaluated prefixes from certified suffixes;
- organizing numerical evidence.

However, a trajectory merge proves convergence only when the merged suffix has already been established by exact descent or induction. The geometric representation does not replace the symbolic first-descent proof.

# 11. Computational Evidence

The LNL/LZR proof-oriented analyzer records:

- the first lower odd iterate;
- accelerated and ordinary steps to first descent;
- the trajectory peak before first descent;
- the valuation sequence \(v_2(3n+1)\);
- cumulative halving exponents;
- exact affine constants;
- trailing-one corridor lengths;
- residue classes;
- exact identity checks.

The project has exhaustively tested millions of finite starts without finding a first-descent failure, and the exact affine identities were independently reproduced.

These results support the framework and test the implementation. They do not prove the universal statement because every finite verification leaves infinitely many integers untested.

# 12. Current Proof Status

The following components are established:

- the first-descent reduction theorem;
- immediate descent for all odd integers \(n\equiv1\pmod4\);
- the trailing-one growth-corridor lemma;
- the exact affine iteration identity;
- the exact descent inequality;
- the formal residue-tree closure rules;
- exact computational verification for large finite ranges;
- independent reproduction of selected results.

The following component remains open:

> Construct a finite symbolic residue-covering certificate that closes every odd residue family.

Until this certificate is produced and independently verified, the Lange Number Line project constitutes a rigorous proof program, but not a completed proof of the Collatz conjecture.
